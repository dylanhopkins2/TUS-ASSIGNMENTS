Best Destinations to Visit

Hella, Iceland

Iceland’s brisk Nordic air makes for skies that are so clear, you’ll see millions of stars overhead. Hella is not only home to great skies for stargazing, but also to the otherworldly Northern Lights as well. Located further south than other similar Northern Lights viewpoints, you’ll be able to stay outdoors longer, which is good news – with double the sights, you’ll want to make sure you don’t miss anything.

La Palma, Canary Islands

La Palma in the Canary Islands is well-known as one of the best spots for stargazing due to its exceptionally clear skies. Its unique position in the Atlantic Ocean gives it a natural cloud screen that reflects sunlight away from the sky above. It’s also close to the equator, which allows stargazers to see stars from both the northern and southern hemispheres.

Kerry, Ireland

Kerry International Dark Sky Reserve sits on the Iveragh Peninsula, home to nearly 4,000 residents, and incorporates approximately 700 square km of territory along the Wild Atlantic Way tourism trail. Its location between the Kerry Mountains and the Atlantic Ocean provides some natural protection against light pollution.

Beskydy Dark-Sky Park, Czech Republic, Slovakia & Poland

The region of eastern Czech Republic that borders Slovakia, the Beskydy Protected Landscape Area, is mostly mountains covered in indigenous primeval forests. It’s possible to take part in various stargazing activities in the mountainous setting of Gruň, including a ‘relaxation under the stars’ sky tour.

Haunte-Pyrénées, France

Haute-Pyrénées is a region in the south of France just off the Pyrénées Mountains that separate France from Spain. There, you’ll find the Pyrénées National Park, which is the biggest International Dark Sky Reserve in Europe and home to the Pic du Midi Observatory.

Westhavelland, Germany

Westhavelland is a nature park that is recognized as a Dark Sky Reserve by the International Dark-Sky Association. Its certified dark skies are protected from light pollution from major population centers that are nearby, and its proximity to Berlin makes this designation extra special. This also makes Westhavelland one of the more accessible Dark Sky Reserves.

Tuscany, Italy

There’s no better place to take up astronomy than where Galileo developed his telescope, and Tuscany’s dry weather and distance from urban centers makes for perfect stargazing conditions. Here, you can see the moon’s craters, sunspots, and Jupiter’s moons.

Hortobágy National Park, Hungary

Hortobágy National Park is a UNESCO World Heritage site, the first Hungarian National Park, and an IDA-recognized Dark Sky Park. It’s one of the biggest continuous grasslands, and its different terrain and water sources make it a great home to more than 340 different species of bird
